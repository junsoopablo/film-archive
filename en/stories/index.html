Manufacturer Stories

The People Who Made the Projectors

Where did the projectors in this collection come from? A precision engineer in Stuttgart, a 13-year-old school dropout in Saint-Étienne, a lens craftsman in Japan — their machines brought cinema to theaters and homes around the world.

Germany · Stuttgart 1905–1985

Bauer

"Every other film shown in cinemas and on television today is projected by a Bauer machine."

In 1905, precision engineer Eugen Bauer started his business. His first 35mm projector in 1907 featured a take-up spool that automatically wound film after projection — while French competitors still let film pile into baskets, requiring manual rewinding.

In 1919, young mechanic Christian Wacker joined and contributed key ideas for the next generation. In 1925, Bauer produced the world's first 'left-hand 35mm projector,' allowing a single projectionist to operate two machines side by side — a design that remains the cinema standard today.

By the 1930s, Bauer developed optical sound systems (LT3) for the talkie era. After merging with Bosch in 1974, the company proudly claimed that half of all films shown in cinemas and on TV were projected by Bauer machines.

In this collection: Approximately 30 Bauer projectors (P6, P7, T1, T2, T190, Pantalux, etc.)
France · Paris 1896–1970s

Pathé

A film empire born from Edison's phonograph demonstrations

Charles Pathé (born 1863) began earning money by demonstrating Edison phonographs at fairs near Paris in 1894. This was the beginning of what would become one of the world's largest film companies.

Pathé's most innovative invention was the 1912 KOK 28mm projector. In an era when 35mm nitrate film was too dangerous for home use, Pathé developed 28mm 'safety film,' opening the door for cinema to leave the theater and enter the home for the first time.

Pathé later invented the 9.5mm format, further democratizing home cinema. The 9.5mm film featured unique center perforations between frames, providing a wider image. Dozens of models followed — Pathé Baby, Kid, Vox — before Kodak acquired the company in 1927.

In this collection: Approximately 15 Pathé projectors/cameras (KOK 28mm, Baby, 9.5mm series)
Germany · Dresden 1926–1970s

Zeiss Ikon

How a farmer's son came to dominate 90% of the world's flight recorder market

At the 1935 Berlin electronics fair, engineer Heinz Thiele first saw the AEG Magnetophon K1 tape recorder — and sound waves followed him for life. By 1950, he headed sound technology at Zeiss Ikon, creating the Movilux, Moviphon, and Movivox series.

Thiele discovered a young talent: Hans F. Naepfel, 'a farmer's son with only elementary education.' Starting as an unskilled worker assembling parts, Naepfel received Thiele's mentorship and eventually joined Fairchild, where he came to control 90% of the world's flight recorder (black box) market.

In 1958, Thiele developed the Moviton, the first 8mm sound projector capable of recording magnetic tracks on film edges. But management refused production — 'too expensive and complex.' As Naepfel later recalled: 'The old executives loved their Compur shutters. Then in 1958, the Japanese overtook us with modern technology.'

In this collection: Approximately 10 Zeiss Ikon projectors/cameras (TK 35mm, Movilux, Cronos)
France · Saint-Étienne 1930–1981

Heurtier

A 13-year-old dropout who became an inventor with 65 patents

Antoine Heurtier was a church choir singer and theater performer at 12, a school dropout at 13. After working at 24 different factories in Saint-Étienne, the self-taught mechanic started his own business at 28.

The turning point came from his father: 'Shall I buy a little projector for the grandchildren?' But the purchased projector broke after just 8 days, and the salesman said: 'I can't give you another — they're all the same low quality.' That moment, Heurtier decided to build his own.

His 'Trifilm' was revolutionary — one projector that could play 9.5mm, 16mm, and 8mm film. Orders arrived within 8 days. 'It was the finest device on the world market,' he said passionately.

In 1981, his patents expired and 'Japanese companies immediately copied everything at 40% cheaper prices.' The company closed, but Swiss Bolex Paillard had purchased Heurtier's patents — recognition that the self-taught man without credentials had earned his place.

In this collection: Approximately 5 Heurtier projectors (Super-Tri, Stereo 42)
Germany · Munich 1928–1980

Agfa

"Toy of the year" — the devastating review that sealed its last invention

The first Agfa 16mm projector, the Movector 16A, was born in 1928 at the Munich factory. The 1935 Movector Upper 16 was marketed as 'a projector for the upper class,' featuring a 375W lamp and 100-250V AC/DC compatibility.

During WWII, Agfa's 106,000m² Munich factory became an air raid target. As men were sent to the front, women worked alongside foreign forced laborers, and the company renamed its production 'kriegswichtiger Produktion' (war-important production).

In 1980, Agfa launched its final invention: the 'Family System.' A disc-shaped camera with a tiny built-in screen projector, priced at $1,000. German magazine Foto-Magazin called it 'toy of the year.' It was the last film projector ever made by the global giant.

In this collection: Approximately 10 Agfa projectors (Movector, Sonector series)
Germany · Berlin 1932–1968

Siemens

The green enamel Siemens 2000 — the projector that became a school standard

In 1932, German electrical giant Siemens began producing 16mm cameras at its Wernerwerk factory in Berlin-Siemensstadt. Projectors followed a year later.

In 1928, Siemens and AEG jointly founded Klangfilm ('Sound-Film') to share sound film technology expertise. Siemens acquired full control in 1941.

The 1952 Siemens 2000 became iconic — a hammered green enamel 16mm projector that served as the standard in schools and small cinemas for years. With lamps from 250W to 1,000W and a universal AC/DC motor, these machines are still found in working condition today.

When Siemens ceased camera production in 1960, its film equipment division was sold to Bosch/Bauer. The last Siemens projector, the 3008, was briefly reborn as the Bauer T30.

In this collection: Approximately 10 Siemens projectors (16mm, 2000 series)
Switzerland 1923–1980s

Bolex (Paillard)

Founded by an 'ambitious, mysterious self-made man'

In 1923, Jaques Bogopolsky — described as 'an ambitious, mysterious self-made man' — founded Compagnie Bol S.A in Switzerland. The company was later acquired by precision instrument maker Paillard.

The Paillard-Bolex H16 camera became legendary. In projectors, the 1949 M8 series sold 158,000 units. The innovative 1968 Multimatic featured automatic cartridge-based film loading.

Bolex projector production eventually passed to Austria's Eumig, which manufactured roughly 200,000 sound and 120,000 silent projectors under the Bolex name. The era ended with Eumig's bankruptcy.

In this collection: Approximately 8 Bolex projectors/cameras (18-5, H16, M8 series)
USA · Chicago 1907–1979

Bell & Howell

90% of all professional films were perforated by their machines

In 1910, 27-year-old Donald Bell and 28-year-old Albert Howell partnered to improve projector technology. Howell was the first to recognize the importance of industry standards. His 1910 perforator was so precise that a 1942 survey found '90% of professional films were perforated by Bell & Howell machines.'

The 1933 Filmosound projector became the company's most important product — a bestseller for over 40 years. By 1948, 105,000 units had been sold, and a Filmosound Library catalog offered 1,700 film titles for rental.

In 1960, Bell & Howell partnered with Japan's Osawa (Mamiya), building a factory near Tokyo. But as film technology became unprofitable in 1974, the company sold its factories and trademark to Osawa in 1979, exiting the film equipment business entirely.

In this collection: Approximately 5 Bell & Howell projectors/cameras
Japan 1948–1986

Chinon

60,000 cameras a month — but nobody knew their name

In 1948, Hiroshi Chino founded Chinon. The company's fate was OEM manufacturing — its machines carried dozens of other brand names: GAF, Revue, Sears, Bolex, Bauer, Noris, Hanimex, Braun, Eumig, Rollei. Until 1974, the Chinon name was unknown to the public.

By 1975, producing 60,000 movie cameras per month, Chinon was the world's largest manufacturer. That same year, it finally entered the German market under its own brand. But in projectors, it couldn't compete with German and Austrian rivals.

After the film market crash of 1981, Chinon continued Super 8 production until 1986, then pivoted to autofocus lenses and digital cameras before being acquired by Kodak.

In this collection: Multiple Chinon OEM projectors (under Revue, Noris brands)
Austria · Vienna 1840–1956

Ditmar

From lamp factory to dual-format projector innovation

In 1840, Karl Rudolf Ditmar founded a lamp factory in Vienna. Forty years later, 900 employees produced 2 million lamps annually. After merging with Brunner Brothers in 1907, 'Ditmar Brunner AG' was born.

In 1936, Ditmar introduced a revolutionary concept: a single projector that could play two different film formats. With a simple four-step conversion, users could switch between 8mm and 16mm (or 9.5mm). The company marketed it as 'not just another new model, but the perfect projector that never existed before.'

Features included reverse projection, still frames, flicker-free images, 100-175W lamps, and a universal AC/DC motor. The machines were reintroduced after WWII but disappeared after 1956.

In this collection: Ditmar projectors (Model 2860, etc.)
Czechoslovakia · Přerov 1933–present

Meopta

Czech optical excellence born in a 3×5 meter workshop

In 1930, Jindrich Suchanek began making school laboratory equipment in a tiny 3×5 meter workshop. He soon moved to 16mm projectors — his apartment-made machines produced brighter images than expensive foreign competitors.

By 1935, with 17 employees in a textile factory, he produced the ALMO — remarkably similar to Emel and Bell & Howell projectors. Nationalized in 1948 as Meopta, the company produced everything from 35mm theater projectors to amateur 8mm models.

Notably, Meopta never produced a single amateur sound projector — a reflection of socialist Czechoslovakia's industrial priorities.

Today, Meopta occupies 135,000m² with 2,400 employees, known for military optics and riflescopes.

In this collection: Approximately 5 Meopta projectors (Meoclub 16, AM 8)
Japan · Nagoya 1921–present

Elmo

The projectors that command top prices among collectors

Founded in 1921 by Hidenobu Sakaki in Nagoya, Elmo's name stands for Electricity, Light, Machine, Organization. Starting with 35mm projectors, the company expanded to 16mm in 1930.

Elmo's masterpiece was the 1978 GS-1200 Stereo: four DC motors, stereo sound, 360m reels. This projector still commands the highest prices on the used market today. It was distributed worldwide through Honeywell and Ilford.

Elmo survived the decline of film equipment. Today, as part of the Toshiba group, approximately 500 employees manufacture CCD cameras, LCD projectors, and slide scanners — carrying the projector maker's DNA into the digital age.

In this collection: Approximately 3 Elmo projectors
Japan · Tokyo 1946–present

Sekonic / Copal

From shutter maker to projector maker, and back to shutters

Founded in 1946 as a still camera shutter manufacturer, Copal became Copal Koki Company in 1949 and moved to Tokyo in 1954. Under the Sekonic brand, it gained worldwide fame for light meters.

In 1958, Sekonic entered the projector market with a metal 8mm machine priced at 16,000 yen. Through the 1970s, the company produced dual-format Super 8 / standard 8mm projectors and OEM-supplied to Rollei and Voigtlander.

Today, Copal belongs to Japan's Nidec Group, manufacturing shutters, motor drives, sensors, LEDs, and lenses — still in the world of precision optical machinery where it began.

In this collection: Sekonic projectors in collection
